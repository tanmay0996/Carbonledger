## SAP Flat File Export

What I looked at: SAP ALV report output documentation, SAP SE standard SLIS reporting format, SAP MM goods movement (transaction MIGO, movement type 201/261) documentation, and public SAP community posts about flat file CO2 reporting interfaces.

What I learned: SAP uses semicolons as column delimiters in German locale environments because commas are the decimal separator (500.000 means five hundred, not five). Column headers appear in German in the default export. Buchungskreis is company code, Werk is plant, Materialnummer is material number, Menge is quantity, Einheit is unit of measure (Einheit der Menge), Buchungsdatum is the posting date. Dates come out as YYYYMMDD with no separators -- this is the SAP internal date format and never changes regardless of locale. Movement type 201 is the code for goods issue to cost center, which is the standard movement for consumable fuel. Plant codes are numeric and four digits by default (1000, 2000) though clients often create their own.

Why the sample data looks the way it does: Two company codes (1000, 2000) reflect a realistic two-entity corporate structure. Plants PLANT_1000 through PLANT_5000 map to named German cities in the lookup table, which is how a real integration would work -- SAP stores the plant code, not the location name, so you maintain a side table. Material codes use MAT-TYPE-NNN to make them identifiable at a glance. Units mix L, KG, and GAL because fuel procurement in SAP genuinely mixes these: US-sourced diesel is often invoiced in gallons, heavy fuel oil is sold by weight, and standard diesel and petrol are in liters. Dirty rows include a missing company code, a missing plant, a non-numeric quantity, an invalid date (99991301, which fails the YYYYMMDD parse), and an unsupported unit (BARREL) to show the parser handles each case cleanly.

What would break in production: The biggest issue is column mapping. Different SAP ALV reports and different SAP versions export columns in different orders, and column names can be renamed by the client's BASIS team. The parser matches by header name to handle this, but if the client has renamed Buchungsdatum to PostingDate or Menge to Quantity, the parser would fail to find those columns. A production deployment would need a per-client column mapping configuration file. The second issue is character encoding -- SAP German locale exports use CP1252 or ISO-8859-1, not UTF-8. The parser assumes UTF-8. If the file is not re-encoded before upload, German umlauts in Materialbezeichnung (material description) will corrupt. Third, the material-to-fuel-type mapping table is hardcoded. Real clients have thousands of material codes and the relevant fuel materials need to be identified from their material master data, not assumed from a short list. Fourth, SAP can export goods movements in multiple currency and UoM variants depending on the valuation area setup. A row might have a base unit of measure (KG) different from the order unit (L), and only one of them appears in the flat file export depending on which ALV variant was used.

## Utility Portal Export

What I looked at: E.ON Business portal export documentation, Vattenfall German commercial portal, EnBW energy data export format, and Ofgem smart meter CSV export guidance for the UK.

What I learned: Utility portals aimed at commercial customers export by billing period, not calendar month. Billing periods are set when the meter is commissioned and are based on the meter read cycle -- typically 28-31 days, but not aligned to the 1st of the month unless the customer requested it. Industrial customers with high-voltage connections (HV tariff) often have monthly reads aligned to calendar months, while standard commercial meters do not. The demand_kw column (maximum demand in kW) appears on commercial and industrial invoices as a basis for capacity charges, but not on small business or residential bills. Meter IDs on portal exports are the Distribution Network Operator (DNO) Meter Point Reference Number (MPRN) in the UK or the Zählpunkt in Germany, not the physical meter serial number.

Why the sample data looks the way it does: Sites include both calendar-aligned (e.g. MTR-001 Jan 1 to Jan 31) and non-calendar periods (e.g. MTR-004 Jan 15 to Feb 14) to exercise the cross-month detection. MTR-009 Bremen Terminal has the highest usage (100k+ kWh/month) to reflect an industrial terminal load, while office sites are lower. One row has no meter ID (blank) and one has no usage value to trigger the two failure modes the parser handles. Tariff types are drawn from real German categories: commercial_standard, industrial_high_voltage, industrial_standard, commercial_off_peak.

What would break in production: The emission factor (0.207 kgCO2e/kWh) is the UK grid average for 2023-24 from the National Grid ESO. This is wrong for Germany (roughly 0.385 kgCO2e/kWh in 2023), France (0.052 kgCO2e/kWh, mostly nuclear), and every other country. A production system needs a lookup table keyed by country and year, updated annually. Second, real utility exports vary significantly in column names and date formats -- E.ON uses DD.MM.YYYY, EnBW uses YYYY-MM-DD, and some portals export dates as Unix timestamps. The parser currently requires ISO dates (YYYY-MM-DD). Third, some portals include reactive power, power factor, and interval data (15-minute reads) in the same export file. The parser would need to detect and ignore those rows. Fourth, multisite customers may get a single consolidated export where one file covers hundreds of meters across different countries, each needing a different emission factor.

## Corporate Travel Export (Concur-style)

What I looked at: SAP Concur standard expense report export documentation (Concur Travel & Expense, the SAP-owned platform), the Concur TripLink API schema, Concur's standard report field reference, and DEFRA greenhouse gas reporting conversion factors 2023 for business travel.

What I learned: Concur's default export from the Expense module is cost-focused: expense type, amount, merchant, receipt status, approval status. It does not include travel-specific fields like origin airport, destination airport, or distance. Those fields come from the Travel module (booking data) and require TripLink to connect travel bookings with expense reports. Many corporate Concur setups do not have TripLink configured, which means the travel team has to manually annotate itinerary data or the company relies on the travel management company (TMC) to provide a separate report. This is why flight IATA codes appear without distance in a lot of real exports -- someone exported the booking data without the segment distance field. Hotel emissions are calculated per night, not per km, because hotels are stationary. DEFRA 2023 factors: flight economy average 0.255 kgCO2e/passenger-km, car rental average 0.171 kgCO2e/km, rail 0.041 kgCO2e/km (UK average), hotel 31 kgCO2e/night (average business hotel).

Why the sample data looks the way it does: Trip IDs use TRP-NNNNN, which matches the sequence format Concur assigns to expense reports. Employee IDs are anonymized to EMP-NNNN as required by GDPR for any cross-system data. Routes are realistic intercontinental (FRA-JFK, SIN-NRT) and European (MUC-LHR, BER-AMS) city pairs reflecting a German headquartered company with international business travel. Several flight rows have no distance_km because the booking system only provided the IATA codes, triggering the haversine fallback. Hotel rows use the origin city as the hotel location. One row has a missing origin and destination (row TRP-10034) and one has an unrecognized employee ID format (row TRP-10033) to show dirty data handling.

What would break in production: The haversine lookup table covers 17 airports. A real deployment needs the full IATA database (~9,000 airports). Routes through airports not in the table would fail with "unknown airport codes" and require manual entry or a distance API call. Second, Concur column names are configurable -- an administrator can rename any column in the report template, so "travel_type" might be called "Expense Type" or "Category" depending on the company's configuration. A real integration needs a column mapping step before parsing. Third, the emission factors are per-passenger-km for economy class. Business class flights have a factor roughly 2-3x higher per DEFRA guidance (due to seat area and load factor adjustments). The current parser applies a single flight factor regardless of booking class, which will undercount business class emissions significantly. The booking_class column is in the sample data but not used. Fourth, rail emission factors vary enormously by country -- UK rail averages 0.041 kgCO2e/km, French rail is near zero (electric, nuclear-powered), and German rail sits around 0.030 kgCO2e/km. Using the UK factor for all rail travel produces wrong numbers for non-UK companies.
